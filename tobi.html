Water

The water shader is usually the most complicated piece of tech art in all of my games, and ToBI was no exception. ToBI's water is a set of shaders that are responsible for a significant amount of color design and simulation.

Making things even more complicated, the open-world nature of the game meant that the same material needed to look convincing and non-repetitive at very different scales — both when the camera was practically rubbing against it, and when viewed from the top of a building several kilometers away.

Let's go through the solution step by step!

Modeling

The ocean in ToBI signifies the natural end of the playspace, and was specifically modeled to match up with the various beaches and buildings along that edge. While this required manual work of matching up vertices and shapes, it ended up being necessary for performance (I didn't want a high-impact water plane being rendered underneath everything in the city) and for manually defining where I wanted the most detailed tesselation.

Of course, you can also slap the material on a procedural polygon, which was useful for making swimming pools, fountains, aqueducts, and whatever else.

Level of Detail

Obviously, nobody has the VRAM to simulate an entire ocean at this kind of resolution, so this is where LOD tricks came into play. After a certain distance, the detailed displacement-and-velocity "near simulation" starts to fade into a much cheaper "far simulation", which is basically just the classic "scroll some scaled normal maps over each other" trick for making water. This is the version of the water you're looking at from the tops of buildings.

Interestingly, this also ended up being one of those situations where putting an execution branch in a shader did more good than harm. Evaluating the near simulation is extremely expensive per-pixel, and having the shader completely bypass those lines upon reaching the far LOD made the shader much more performant, given how much distant ocean is usually onscreen.

While I'm still proud of what I was able to accomplish with this material, I've found myself wishing I had done more with surface foam and impact particles for objects entering and leaving. That'll be a priority for future games.

Explosions

What kind of 3D adventure game would ToBI be without red, beeping crates that explode? This effect is created by a very specific particle system and a very specific shader working in tandem.

For some added fun, Luke's shader also has a "soot damage" float uniform, which gets turned up when this damage type is detected and then decreased over time. Toasty!

User Interface

ToBI's art direction is a sun-drenched, graffitipunk take on the Memphis Style — kind of like Sunset Overdrive meets 90s Nickelodeon bumpers, but with more pastels. The operating word was "fluid" — when elements from the UI appear and disappear, they get spray-painted onto the screen and fade out in a similar way.

Zap

One fun effect that ended up going unused was an implementation of the quintessential "x-ray skeleton" effect for Luke getting electrocuted, which was inspired by a similar effect in Super Mario Galaxy.

I modeled Luke's skeleton (to.. an extent), made it glow, and also wrote a special shader / material for his body that flips the culling and applies a matcap-style glow. The glow is an addition of two dot products of the camera vector and the normal — a soft glow when they're near-parallel, and a bright glow when they're near-perpendicular.

This effect is actually still 100% functional in the final build — Luke's skeleton is always there, lurking — but none of the enemies or hazards in the game ended up using the "Electric" damage type, so it's impossible to trigger. Game development!
